# Listicle hooks — 12 patterns

Listicle lives or dies on the first 1.5 seconds. The hook does three jobs at once: (1) state the count, (2) state the topic specifically, (3) plant the open loop on #1. If any of the three is missing, retention drops.

Each pattern below has the same shape:
- **Setup** — what to say (template).
- **Why it works** — the retention mechanic.
- **Example** — a filled-in line.

You can mix and match — most strong hooks combine two of these patterns.

---

## 1. "Top N <thing> you didn't know"

**Setup.** "Top {N} {topic} that {surprising-claim}."

**Why it works.** "Didn't know" is a curiosity gap. The viewer is auto-recruited as someone who wants to find out if they're in the in-group or the out-group. Search-friendly — "didn't know" pulls long-tail traffic on YouTube.

**Example.** "Top 5 AI tools you didn't know are free."

---

## 2. "N <X> that will <benefit>"

**Setup.** "{N} {topic} that will {clear-benefit-in-future-tense}."

**Why it works.** Future-tense benefit = implied result. Concrete benefit ("save you 10 hours a week") beats vague ("change your life") by a wide margin.

**Example.** "5 productivity habits that will give you back 10 hours a week."

---

## 3. "I tested N <X>, here's #1"

**Setup.** "I tested {N} {topic}. Here's the one that actually worked."

**Why it works.** First-person testing = credibility. "Here's #1" puts the open loop in the hook itself — viewer must stay to find out which one. Works especially well for tools, foods, and methods.

**Example.** "I tested 7 AI note-taking apps for a month. Here's the one I'm still paying for."

---

## 4. "N controversial <opinions>"

**Setup.** "{N} controversial {topic} {audience} doesn't want to hear."

**Why it works.** Controversy = comments engine, comments = algorithmic boost. Adding "doesn't want to hear" frames the viewer as in on the secret, not the target.

**Example.** "3 controversial money rules every personal-finance guru gets wrong."

---

## 5. "N things to stop doing"

**Setup.** "{N} {topic} you need to stop doing in {year}."

**Why it works.** Negative framing has higher click-through than positive equivalents on Shorts (loss-aversion). "{Year}" adds urgency and freshness — the same hook with "in 2026" outperforms the version without a year.

**Example.** "3 things you need to stop doing on LinkedIn in 2026."

---

## 6. "N <X> ranked from worst to best"

**Setup.** "{N} {topic} ranked from worst to best."

**Why it works.** Ranking is the literal contract of the format. "Worst to best" = built-in countdown, no narrative work needed. The promise of the best at #1 is automatic.

**Example.** "5 AI image models ranked from worst to best."

---

## 7. "N <X> nobody talks about"

**Setup.** "{N} {topic} nobody is talking about."

**Why it works.** Insider framing — viewer is recruited as part of a small in-group. Pairs well with niche topics (underrated tools, hidden destinations, undervalued skills). Beats generic "best of" hooks by a wide margin in less crowded niches.

**Example.** "5 European cities to visit in winter that nobody is talking about."

---

## 8. "If I were starting <X> today, I'd do these N things"

**Setup.** "If I were starting {topic} today, here are the {N} things I'd do."

**Why it works.** Hindsight framing = authority + relatability. The viewer thinks: "I'm starting this *now*, this is for me." Particularly strong in finance, business, and skill-acquisition niches.

**Example.** "If I were starting a YouTube channel today, here are the 5 things I'd do differently."

---

## 9. "N <X> under $<price>"

**Setup.** "{N} {topic} under ${price}."

**Why it works.** Concrete price constraint = filter the viewer can't argue with. Works for products, services, gifts, travel, food. The constraint is the hook's promise.

**Example.** "5 dinner ideas under $7 that don't taste like a budget meal."

---

## 10. "N signs you're <state>"

**Setup.** "{N} signs you're {desirable-or-undesirable-state}."

**Why it works.** Self-diagnostic format — every item is a "is this me?" check. Pulls viewers all the way through because they're scoring themselves. Strong for personal-development, finance, relationships.

**Example.** "5 signs you're already ahead of 90% of people your age financially."

---

## 11. "N <X> in <unexpected-modifier>"

**Setup.** "{N} {topic} in {unexpected-place / time / constraint}."

**Why it works.** The unexpected modifier creates pattern-break — the brain notices the mismatch and pauses. Travel, food, and tech niches use this constantly.

**Example.** "5 things to eat in Tokyo for under $5 — at 3 AM."

---

## 12. "Don't do these N <X>"

**Setup.** "Don't make these {N} {topic} mistakes."

**Why it works.** Negative imperative is a stronger stop-scroll than the positive ("Do these 5 things"). Works because mistake-avoidance has higher perceived value than gain ("avoiding loss > acquiring equivalent gain"). Particularly potent for finance, fitness, relationships.

**Example.** "Don't make these 3 mistakes when you're choosing your first credit card."

---

## Mix-and-match

Strong hooks combine two patterns. Examples:

- **#1 + #4** ("Top 5 controversial AI tools nobody talks about")
- **#3 + #6** ("I tested 7 budget meal kits — here they are, ranked from worst to best")
- **#5 + #9** ("3 things to stop buying that cost you under $20 a month — but $200 a year")

Don't stack three. The hook gets confused.

## Anti-patterns (don't ship these)

- "Top {very-large-N}" — anything ≥10 in a 60s video is too thin per item. Use 5 or 7.
- "Best {topic} of all time" — too generic, no open loop, kills retention.
- "{N} things" with no topic — "5 things you should know" alone is meaningless. Always specify the topic.
- Promising "you won't believe #3" — algorithm-flagged clickbait phrasing. Use "you didn't know" instead.
