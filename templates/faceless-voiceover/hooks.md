# Hooks — faceless-voiceover

The hook is the entire format's load-bearing element. 0-2 seconds, one sentence, falsifiable and counter-intuitive. If the hook is vague, the rest of the video doesn't matter — the viewer already scrolled.

Pick the pattern by niche. Combine with a topic. Verify the claim is true before shipping — a wrong hook torches the channel's authority on the first watch.

## Pattern 1 — "Did you know that <X>?"

> "Did you know that Napoleon was actually taller than the average Frenchman of his time?"

- **Setup.** A common belief is wrong. State the corrected version as the hook.
- **Why.** "Did you know" is the canonical curiosity primer. The brain auto-answers "no, tell me", and the watch-time is locked.
- **Niche fit.** History (top), science-trivia, mythology. Avoid for finance and tech (sounds soft).

## Pattern 2 — "Here's why <X> is actually <Y>"

> "Here's why your savings account is actually losing you money."

- **Setup.** Reframe a thing the viewer thinks they understand.
- **Why.** Implies privileged knowledge. The "actually" is the curiosity trigger — promises a reframe.
- **Niche fit.** Finance (top), psychology, tech. Works in history with caution.

## Pattern 3 — "Most people don't realize that <X>"

> "Most people don't realize that the dollar bill in their wallet has a hidden code."

- **Setup.** "Most people" puts the viewer in the in-group if they keep watching.
- **Why.** Status hook. The viewer wants to be in the 1% who knows.
- **Niche fit.** Universal. Strong for finance, psychology, tech, history.

## Pattern 4 — "The real story of <X>"

> "The real story of how Coca-Cola almost killed itself with one product."

- **Setup.** Implies the version the viewer knows is incomplete or wrong.
- **Why.** Combines authority ("real") with promise of a payoff. Best for narrative-heavy topics.
- **Niche fit.** History (top), true-crime, finance (corporate stories).

## Pattern 5 — "What <experts/scientists/historians> won't tell you about <X>"

> "What historians won't tell you about the Great Wall of China."

- **Setup.** Conspiracy-light framing. The viewer feels like they're getting insider info.
- **Why.** Cheap trust hack — works once per video. Don't lean on this for every video, the channel reads as click-bait.
- **Niche fit.** History, science-trivia, finance. Avoid for tech (sounds tinfoil).

## Pattern 6 — "In <year>, this happened" (date-specific)

> "In 1907, a man bought the entire Manhattan island for $24."

- **Setup.** A specific year and a specific event. Concrete > abstract.
- **Why.** Specificity is curiosity-rocket-fuel. The brain wants the context.
- **Niche fit.** History (top), true-crime (cases), finance (market crashes), tech (founding moments).

## Pattern 7 — "X people died because of <Y>"

> "Eight people died because of a typo in a 1985 weather report."

- **Setup.** A specific number + a consequence + a cause that doesn't match the consequence.
- **Why.** Mortality + mismatch = top-tier curiosity. The "Y" must feel disproportionate.
- **Niche fit.** History, true-crime (top), tech (engineering disasters), science-trivia.

## Pattern 8 — "The reason <common-thing> exists is <unexpected-cause>"

> "The reason your phone has a SIM card is because of a German lab experiment in 1991."

- **Setup.** A mundane object with a surprising origin.
- **Why.** Origin stories are evergreen. The hook promises a "huh, never thought about that" moment.
- **Niche fit.** Tech (top), history, science-trivia, mythology.

## Pattern 9 — "If <X>, you'd be <Y>"

> "If you'd invested $100 in Apple in 1980, you'd have $2.4 million today."

- **Setup.** Counterfactual + concrete number. The viewer projects themselves into the alternate timeline.
- **Why.** Loss aversion + curiosity. They want to know how the rest of the math plays out.
- **Niche fit.** Finance (top), history, tech. Strong for psychology if framed around a decision.

## Pattern 10 — "There's a country/place where <X>"

> "There's a country where it's illegal to die."

- **Setup.** Geography-anchored absurdity. The viewer can't predict where it's going.
- **Why.** "Illegal to die" can't be true literally — the viewer has to keep watching to resolve the contradiction.
- **Niche fit.** History, mythology, science-trivia (top). Works for true-crime as legal-system-trivia.

## Pattern 11 — "<X> is the most <superlative> thing in <category>"

> "The blue whale's heart is the loudest thing in the ocean."

- **Setup.** A clean superlative. No qualifier, no hedge.
- **Why.** Superlatives are absolute claims — the brain wants the proof.
- **Niche fit.** Science-trivia (top), history, finance (biggest trade ever, etc.).

## Pattern 12 — "Why does <common-thing> <weird-behavior>?"

> "Why does honey never go bad?"

- **Setup.** Question hook. Pose a thing the viewer never thought to ask.
- **Why.** The question itself is the payoff promise. Once asked, the brain demands the answer.
- **Niche fit.** Science-trivia (top), psychology, tech, history.

## Hook-quality checklist

Before shipping, the hook must satisfy all four:

- [ ] **Specific.** Names a year, a number, a place, or a named entity. Not "in the past" — "in 1907".
- [ ] **Falsifiable.** Could be true or false. "Happiness comes from within" is a vibe, not a hook.
- [ ] **Counter-intuitive.** Goes against the default expectation. If the viewer's reaction is "yeah, obviously", it failed.
- [ ] **Verifiable.** The agent can confirm the claim with a citation in `<60s` of search. Wrong facts torch trust on the first watch.

If any box is unchecked, regenerate the hook. Don't ship the video on a soft hook.
