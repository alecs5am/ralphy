# Real Estate — Opening Hooks (first 2 seconds)

A real estate scroll dies in two seconds. The buyer has 40 listings open in tabs. Your opening must plant a single involuntary thought: *"Wait — where is this?"*

Pick one hook per cut. Don't stack two. The hook is a frame-0 + 1.5s of camera-move commitment, not a montage.

---

## 1. Drone aerial swoop toward facade

**Frame 0**: high overhead, property centered in landscape — neighborhood, ocean, mountain, or skyline visible as context.
**Move (5s clip)**: drone descends and pushes inward toward the front facade or signature architectural feature (a chimney, a glass wall, a courtyard).
**Trigger**: awe + location advantage.
**Best for**: estates with grounds, hillside homes, properties whose location is the asset.
**Reference needed**: aerial or front-elevation photo. If user has no aerial, prompt the keyframe from a high front-elevation photo + sky context.

---

## 2. Front-door reveal (threshold push)

**Frame 0**: heavy front door closed, golden-hour light raking across material grain — wood, oak, painted oxblood, or matte black.
**Move**: door swings inward, camera crosses threshold, soaring foyer or sight-line through to a far view fills frame.
**Trigger**: anticipation + the promise of the inside being as good as the door suggests.
**Best for**: every archetype. The most universally usable hook in the file.
**Reference needed**: front door + foyer / inside-from-foyer photo. Both required to keep the door material and the interior consistent.

---

## 3. Window-view reveal

**Frame 0**: dim interior corner of a room, window visible at the far end with bright value beyond.
**Move**: smooth dolly-in toward the window; the landscape (water / city / mountains / vineyard) gradually fills the frame and the room recedes.
**Trigger**: envy + lifestyle upgrade.
**Best for**: waterfront, skyline penthouses, mountain retreats, vineyard / scenic.
**Reference needed**: photo from inside the room toward the window. The view must be real — never invent a horizon.

---

## 4. Golden-hour facade slow-pan

**Frame 0**: front of property, sun at 30–45° (early evening or early morning), long shadows across stone or stucco, sky in apricot.
**Move**: slow lateral gimbal glide along the facade, light raking across surface texture.
**Trigger**: timelessness, refined taste.
**Best for**: Mediterranean villas, historic homes, mid-century modern, anything where material texture is the selling point.
**Reference needed**: front facade photo, ideally already shot at golden hour or noon (the keyframe model relights).

---

## 5. Infinity pool edge to horizon

**Frame 0**: water surface in the foreground, the pool's far edge just visible, ocean / valley / city sprawling beyond.
**Move**: slow rise; the pool edge dissolves into the horizon line; sky and water merge.
**Trigger**: opulence + leisure fantasy.
**Best for**: luxury homes, resorts, vacation rentals with premium outdoor.
**Reference needed**: pool deck photo + view photo. Both required.

---

## 6. Architectural detail extreme close-up → pull back

**Frame 0**: impossibly close on a single craftsmanship moment — marble veining, walnut grain, a brass hinge, exposed steel beam joint.
**Move**: slow pull back; the detail recedes; the full room context blooms around it.
**Trigger**: craftsmanship signal + "this is not a starter home."
**Best for**: high-end design-forward listings, designer collaborations, historic restorations.
**Reference needed**: detail photo of the actual material, plus the wide shot of the room it's in.

---

## 7. Day-to-night dissolve

**Frame 0**: exterior in late afternoon golden hour.
**Move**: time-lapse compression — sun drops, shadows lengthen, sky deepens, interior lights ignite, accent uplighting on facade comes alive.
**Trigger**: 24-hour appeal + "this property has two faces, both beautiful."
**Best for**: any high-value property; almost mandatory for vacation rentals and entertaining-focused listings.
**Reference needed**: daytime exterior + evening / twilight exterior. If user has no twilight photo, generate the second half from the daytime ref via lighting transform.

---

## 8. Empty-room light play

**Frame 0**: nearly empty room, geometric rectangles of sunlight thrown across hardwood floor by mullioned windows.
**Move**: slow lateral dolly; light shapes track across floor with the camera; dust motes catch in the rays.
**Trigger**: minimalism, possibility, "I can imagine my life here."
**Best for**: modernist properties, high-end restorations, lofts, anything where buyer projection matters more than staging.
**Reference needed**: photo of the empty or near-empty room with directional natural light.

---

## 9. Skyline-from-window (penthouse)

**Frame 0**: floor-to-ceiling glass, viewer positioned just inside the window, city below.
**Move**: micro-dolly forward toward glass, slight tilt down to reveal the height drop and the street far below.
**Trigger**: status + altitude + exclusivity.
**Best for**: high-rise penthouses, urban luxury condos, hotel residences.
**Reference needed**: window-with-skyline photo. The actual skyline shape (Manhattan vs Dubai vs Singapore) must match the listing.

---

## 10. Driveway / approach (first-person arrival)

**Frame 0**: low POV at the foot of a driveway or stone path, property at the far end, mature trees framing.
**Move**: smooth dolly-forward, camera pace matched to walking speed (3–5 mph), property growing in frame.
**Trigger**: arrival, welcome, "this is the moment of coming home."
**Best for**: estates with proper driveways, gated properties, homes where the approach is itself a feature.
**Reference needed**: photo from the start of the driveway or the gate looking in.

---

## 11. Material transformation under light

**Frame 0**: a single surface — backlit onyx, sun-streaked walnut, a marble counter under raking morning light.
**Move**: light direction shifts (or camera moves) so the material's appearance transforms across the clip — translucency emerges, grain pops, a patina shifts.
**Trigger**: design-forward sensibility, tactile beauty.
**Best for**: design-led luxury, hospitality interiors, restaurants, boutique retail.
**Reference needed**: the actual material in the actual property.

---

## 12. Renovation flash (for before/after only)

**Frame 0**: dilapidated original condition — peeling paint, exposed studs, dated avocado-green tile.
**Move (1.5s)**: hard cut or light-flash transition to the same angle, post-renovation, in flattering light.
**Trigger**: transformation + smart-investment signal.
**Best for**: renovation listings, fix-and-flip portfolios, rehab content marketing.
**Reference needed**: matched-angle before/after pair. This is the only hook in the file that requires *two* photos shot from the same camera position.

---

## Picking the right hook

- **If the listing has a view** → hook 1, 3, 5, 9. Lead with the view.
- **If the listing has architectural distinction** → hook 4, 6, 11. Lead with material.
- **If the listing is mid-market without a hero feature** → hook 2 (front door) is the safest. It works on everything.
- **If the listing is a renovation story** → hook 12 is the only correct choice; everything else buries the story.
- **If the listing is a vacation rental** → hook 5 or 7 (pool, day-to-night). Sell the escape.
- **If the listing is commercial / office** → hook 8 (light play) or a clean variant of hook 2 (lobby reveal).

Do not pick a hook that the user does not have reference photos for. The hook fails the moment the AI invents a feature the property doesn't have.
