# Tier List — opening hooks

The hook is 0-2s and has one job: tell the viewer **what's being ranked** and signal **opinions are coming**. The visible empty grid does half the work — the VO and title card do the rest. Below: 12 patterns. Mix and match.

> **Rule of thumb.** If your hook is just "tier list of X", the algorithm scrolls past you. The hook needs friction — a stake, a flex, a threat, a confession, or an explicit invitation to disagree.

---

## 1. The clean declarative

**Setup.** Title card "RANKING [CATEGORY]" + empty grid visible behind it. VO reads the line flat.
**Why.** Search-bait opener. Indexes for "ranking [category]" queries. No friction, but works because the grid does the visual lift.
**Example.** "Ranking every Marvel movie." (Title: RANKING EVERY MARVEL MOVIE)

## 2. The fight-me opener

**Setup.** Title card "[CATEGORY] TIER LIST" with subtitle "(this is gonna piss people off)".
**Why.** Pre-commits to a controversial take. Viewers stay to find the offending pick.
**Example.** "Anime tier list. This is gonna piss people off." (Title: ANIME TIER LIST · PISS PEOPLE OFF EDITION)

## 3. The most-controversial superlative

**Setup.** "Most controversial [category] tier list ever." Hard cut to grid.
**Why.** Superlatives bait engagement. Even people who don't know the niche click to see the chaos.
**Example.** "Most controversial fast-food tier list ever made."

## 4. The personal-stake confession

**Setup.** "I've watched [N] [things] and ranked them all." VO is matter-of-fact.
**Why.** Authority claim + commitment device. The receipts justify the opinions.
**Example.** "I've played every Final Fantasy and ranked them all."

## 5. The challenge / dare

**Setup.** "Restaurant tier list — fight me." or "[Category] tier list. Try me in comments."
**Why.** Explicit invitation to argue. The comment section becomes part of the format.
**Example.** "Fast-food tier list. Fight me in the comments."

## 6. The "you'll hate this"

**Setup.** "You're not going to like where I put [item]."
**Why.** Promises a specific moment of disagreement. Viewer stays to find their grievance.
**Example.** "You're not going to like where I put Hawaiian pizza."

## 7. The reverse-order tease

**Setup.** "Starting from D-tier and working up." Visible empty grid. First item drops into D.
**Why.** Frames the build. Viewer commits to staying for the S-tier reveal at the end.
**Example.** "Dating apps tier list, D-tier first. Buckle up."

## 8. The 2026 trend-hook

**Setup.** "[Category] in 2026 — ranked." or "Ranking every [X] from 2026."
**Why.** Time-stamped = search-friendly + feels current. Indexes for "[category] 2026" queries.
**Example.** "Ranking every AI tool in 2026."

## 9. The "everyone's wrong about" frame

**Setup.** "Everyone's wrong about [category]. Here's the actual tier list."
**Why.** Positions the creator as the authority and primes for contrarian picks.
**Example.** "Everyone's wrong about Studio Ghibli. Here's the actual tier list."

## 10. The "by request" / "you asked" frame

**Setup.** "You asked for it — [category] tier list." (Implies prior video, reuses goodwill.)
**Why.** Works for series. Each new tier list rides the previous one's audience.
**Example.** "You asked for it — anime opening tier list."

## 11. The receipts-up-front

**Setup.** "I ate at [N] [places] this week. Here's the tier list."
**Why.** Effort signal. Viewer trusts the rankings because the work was visible.
**Example.** "I tried 12 ramen spots this week. Tier list time."

## 12. The "this is hot" + spice warning

**Setup.** "Spicy [category] tier list. Don't say I didn't warn you." or "Hot takes only."
**Why.** Sets expectation that controversial picks are coming. Filters for engagement-likely viewers.
**Example.** "Spicy MCU tier list. Hot takes only."

---

## Picking a hook

- **Default.** #2 (fight-me opener) or #3 (most-controversial superlative). Both pre-commit to friction.
- **For evergreen / search.** #1 (clean declarative) or #8 (2026 trend-hook). Optimized for query indexing.
- **For series / batch.** #10 (you-asked) — reuses prior-video audience.
- **For high-effort categories** (food, travel, products tested). #11 (receipts) — rewards the work.
- **Avoid** opening with "Hey guys" / "What's up" — the algorithm cuts those clips off in the first 0.3s. Lead with the noun.
