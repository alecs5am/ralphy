# hooks.md — the 13-pattern library

> The star file of this template. A hook is a *pattern*, not a line of copy. Each pattern below names the trigger, the setup beats, why it works, and a concrete example. Mix at most two — stacking three or more dilutes the punch.

Patterns are grouped by category. Pick the category from the brief's emotional ask (shock, curiosity, controversy, transformation, intimacy), then pick a pattern inside it.

---

## Category 1: Visual-shock

The eye registers the violation in ~200ms — faster than conscious thought. Highest stop-the-scroll rate, lowest narrative depth.

### 1. Impossible Scale
**Setup.** Frame 0: a person next to an object that is 50× its expected size (a fork the size of a couch, a phone the size of a door). Camera pulls back slowly to confirm the absurdity.
**Why.** The brain has a hard prior on object size. Violation = forced re-evaluation of the entire frame.
**Example.** *"Open on a woman pouring coffee from a 6-foot-tall mug into a normal cup. Pull-back at 1.2s reveals her standing on a stepladder. Sound: deep bass hit on the pull-back."*

### 2. Impossible Physics
**Setup.** Frame 0 looks normal. By 1s, something defies gravity, momentum, or causality. Water flowing up. A coin balanced on its edge for 5 seconds. A person walking on a vertical wall.
**Why.** Pattern interrupt at the level of physics — even a child catches it.
**Example.** *"Tight on hands tying a shoelace. At 1.4s the laces float upward and tie themselves. SFX: notification ding the moment they lift."*

### 3. Color Explosion
**Setup.** 0-1.2s desaturated, near-grayscale. At 1.3s, single bright color (neon pink, electric blue) floods the frame. Hard cut, not fade.
**Why.** Hijacks visual cortex. Used by every top creator at least once because it just works.
**Example.** *"Empty gray hallway, slow dolly-in. At 1.5s a person bursts through the wall mid-frame in a hot-pink jumpsuit. Whoosh + electronic stab."*

### 4. Freeze-Frame Break
**Setup.** Normal action plays for 1.2s. Hard freeze for 0.3s — looks like the video glitched. Then unfreeze with a 4× speed ramp.
**Why.** Ambiguity (is this a buffer? a cut? a glitch?) holds the eye through the freeze. The speed ramp pays off the tension.
**Example.** *"Skater approaches a rail. At 1.2s, freeze mid-air. Record-scratch SFX. At 1.6s, unfreeze at 4× speed — landing slammed in 0.3s."*

## Category 2: Voice-first

The hook is in what's said, not shown. Lowest production cost, highest dependence on writing quality.

### 5. The Gatekeep Open
**Setup.** Direct-to-camera, conspiratorial low voice. First three words establish insider status: *"Nobody on TikTok is talking about…"*, *"My friend in [industry] told me…"*, *"This will get me banned but…"*
**Why.** Triggers FOMO and tribal-knowledge bias. Viewer feels chosen.
**Example.** *"Tight close-up, person glances over their shoulder, leans in. 'My ex worked at [X] for 8 years. Here's what they don't tell you about the return policy.' Cut to b-roll at 2.5s."*

### 6. The Skeptic Hook
**Setup.** Open by *agreeing with the doubter*: *"I thought this was BS too…"*, *"I called this a scam for 6 months and then I tried it…"*, *"You're going to roll your eyes but…"*
**Why.** Disarms the audience's defensive reflex. They came skeptical; you arrived skeptical first; now they listen.
**Example.** *"Person rolls eyes at camera. 'No, I'm not buying another productivity app.' Pause. 'Then this happened.' Cut to screen recording at 1.8s."*

### 7. The Direct Question
**Setup.** Eye contact at frame 0, question by 0.7s: *"Be honest — when's the last time you ___?"*, *"Why did nobody warn me about ___?"*, *"What would YOU do if ___?"*
**Why.** Question forces an internal answer. Internal answer = engagement. Engagement = watch-time.
**Example.** *"Person on a couch, looks straight at camera at 0.4s. 'When's the last time you read a book — like, a real one?' Holds eye contact through 2s.*"

## Category 3: Curiosity-gap

Show 60%, withhold 40%. The 40% is what keeps them watching.

### 8. The Half-Reveal
**Setup.** Frame is composed so the most interesting element is just out of frame. Hands hold something we can't quite see. A face reacts to something we can't see. Body language sells the importance of what's hidden.
**Why.** Brains hate incomplete information more than they hate boring information.
**Example.** *"Tight on a person's face — eyes wide, mouth open. Whatever they're staring at is below the bottom of the frame. Held for 2s. Camera tilts down at 2.4s."*

### 9. The After-First Tease
**Setup.** Open on the *result* of a transformation (a clean room, a finished cake, a healed bruise). At 0.8s, hard cut to the *before* state. Now the brain wants to know how A became B.
**Why.** Reverses the natural curiosity vector — instead of "what happens next?", it's "how did we get here?"
**Example.** *"Open on a magazine-perfect kitchen, golden hour. At 0.7s, hard cut: same room, knee-deep in laundry, sink full. Text: 'I had 3 hours.'"*

### 10. The Ladder Reveal
**Setup.** Each cut reveals a little more. Cut 1 (0-0.5s): unrecognizable macro. Cut 2 (0.5-1.0s): wider, hinting. Cut 3 (1.0-1.5s): identity emerges. Cut 4 (1.5-2.0s): full reveal.
**Why.** Each cut is a micro-payoff that promises a bigger one. Engagement compounds.
**Example.** *"Cut 1: ridges of a fingerprint. Cut 2: a thumb. Cut 3: a hand on a steering wheel. Cut 4: a 1972 Ferrari. Ascending tonal music note per cut."*

## Category 4: Controversy

Take a stance the audience expects you NOT to take. Sharpest engagement, sharpest backlash. Use sparingly and only with conviction — fake controversy reads as cringe in 0.5s.

### 11. The Heretical Take
**Setup.** Open by stating a position that violates an obvious tribal norm: *"I think therapy is overrated"*, *"Reading books is mostly performative"*, *"Most fitness influencers can't actually lift"*. Calm, not shouted.
**Why.** Forces every viewer (agreeing or disagreeing) to formulate a counter-argument. They watch to see if you defend it.
**Example.** *"Person sips coffee, calm. 'I think 90% of meditation apps are actively making people worse.' Sip again. Holds eye contact.*"

### 12. The Visible Fail
**Setup.** Open mid-disaster. Something is on fire, broken, spilled, or going wrong. The creator is the cause and is fully aware. No setup, no context — we're already in the wreckage.
**Why.** Schadenfreude + relatability + curiosity. The brain wants to know how badly this ends.
**Example.** *"Open: oven smoke billows, alarm beeping. Person stares at camera, deadpan, holding a cake-shaped charcoal brick. 'So.'*"

## Category 5: Transformation-tease

The promise of change is the hook. The change itself is the payoff. The hook withholds the *how*.

### 13. The Mid-Process Cut
**Setup.** Open *during* a transformation, not before or after. Halfway through the makeover. Mid-build of the LEGO. Halfway through the haircut. The viewer arrives mid-action and has to figure out start and end.
**Why.** Two curiosity gaps for the price of one — what was it before, what will it be after.
**Example.** *"Tight on hands sanding a battered guitar — already half-stripped. Camera doesn't show the headstock or the body shape. Wide reveal at 5s shows it's a 1959 Strat.*"

## Combinations that work

Two patterns layered well:
- **Visual-shock + curiosity-gap** — impossible physics with the cause off-frame (#2 + #8)
- **Skeptic + transformation-tease** — "I thought this was a scam" + show the after first (#6 + #9)
- **Gatekeep + half-reveal** — "Nobody talks about this…" + tight framing hides the subject (#5 + #8)
- **Visible-fail + direct-question** — disaster + "what would YOU have done?" (#12 + #7)

Combinations that don't:
- Two voice-first patterns (gatekeep + skeptic) — same brain region, sounds redundant.
- Three or more visual-shock effects — the eye stops registering shock after the third.
- Direct-question + visible-fail — too much asked of the viewer in the first second.

## When the user's brief doesn't match any pattern

Two options:
1. **Map it.** "Make something that goes viral" → ask one question: shock, curiosity, controversy, intimacy, or transformation? Map the answer to a category, pick the lowest-risk pattern in that category.
2. **Refuse the template.** If the brief is narrative-heavy ("tell my customer's story across 30s"), Social Hook is the wrong tool — recommend `talking-head-rant` or `before-after-product` instead.
