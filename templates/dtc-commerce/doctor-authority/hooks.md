# Doctor Authority — hook bank

12 opening hooks for the 0-3s authority cold open. Each hook locks the white-coat signal, names a profession, and earns the next 25 seconds of attention. Pair the hook to the profession and the niche or the trust signal collapses.

> **Claim discipline.** Every hook below is a structural opener — none of them includes a curative claim. The mechanism + ingredient land in the body, not the hook. If a hook drifts into "cures X" or "fixes Y", rewrite it as "here's what's happening" or "here's what most people get wrong."

---

## 1. "As a [profession], I tell every patient this…"

- **Profession fit:** dermatologist, nutritionist, dentist, GP — universal.
- **Niche:** broadest. Works for any vertical that has patients (skincare, supplements, dental, wellness).
- **Claim discipline:** the hook commits to a recommendation, not a cure. Body must explain the mechanism — otherwise the hook reads as empty authority posturing.
- **Why it works:** "I tell every patient" implies repetition over time → expertise. "This" creates a forward-pointing curiosity gap.

## 2. "If you have [pain state], you need to know this…"

- **Profession fit:** dermatologist (acne / aging), nutritionist (fatigue / bloat), dentist (sensitivity / staining), pharmacist (sleep / anxiety).
- **Niche:** problem-specific verticals — best when the pain state is named precisely ("if your skin is breaking out around your jawline").
- **Claim discipline:** "you need to know" is an information-promise, not a treatment-promise. Body must deliver actual information (mechanism), not just the product name.
- **Why it works:** specificity in the pain state self-selects the high-intent viewer.

## 3. "Most [profession]s don't tell you this…"

- **Profession fit:** any, but lands hardest with dermatologist, dentist, pharmacist (professions the viewer has personal experience with).
- **Niche:** ingredient-led nutra and skincare. Pairs naturally with "lesser-known active" stories (e.g. tranexamic acid, magnesium glycinate).
- **Claim discipline:** the "they don't tell you" frame must be backed by an actual mechanism reveal, not a conspiracy. If the body is just "buy this," the hook backfires as click-bait.
- **Why it works:** insider-knowledge cue + slight contrarian charge → high curiosity. Be careful — easy to drift into anti-medical-establishment territory.

## 4. "Stop using [product category] — here's why…"

- **Profession fit:** dermatologist (cleansers, exfoliants), dentist (whitening strips, hard-bristle brushes), nutritionist (multivitamins, energy drinks).
- **Niche:** category-replacement plays. Best when there's a clear "stop X, switch to Y" story.
- **Claim discipline:** must name a specific failure mechanism in the body, not just "X is bad." Avoid disparaging named brands — go after the category, not the competitor.
- **Why it works:** a stop-command is a strong pattern interrupt. The viewer halts to check if they're guilty.

## 5. "The ingredient your [product category] is missing…"

- **Profession fit:** dermatologist, nutritionist, pharmacist.
- **Niche:** ingredient-led skincare / supplements where one molecule does the heavy lifting (niacinamide, magnesium glycinate, ashwagandha, hyaluronic acid).
- **Claim discipline:** the ingredient must be named in the body within 15s. The hook is a curiosity contract — break it and trust dies.
- **Why it works:** frames the viewer's current product as incomplete, sets up the recommendation as the missing piece.

## 6. "I've prescribed this for [N] years…"

- **Profession fit:** GP, cardiologist, dermatologist (not pharmacist, not nutritionist — they don't prescribe).
- **Niche:** clinical-grade supplements, OTC treatments, established ingredients.
- **Claim discipline:** if the figure is AI-generated, this hook walks closest to the impersonation line. The body must NOT name a real practice or real credentials. "I've prescribed this in my career" stays generic; "I've prescribed this at Mount Sinai" crosses into impersonation.
- **Why it works:** time-on-the-job is the strongest credibility cue an authority figure can offer.

## 7. "If you have [condition], this is the protocol I follow…"

- **Profession fit:** any clinical profession. Strongest with dermatologist, GP, dentist.
- **Niche:** protocol-walkthrough variant — multi-step regimens (acne routine, sleep stack, oral-care order-of-operations).
- **Claim discipline:** "protocol" implies multi-step, so the body MUST deliver 2-4 specific steps. A single product recommendation under a "protocol" framing breaks trust.
- **Why it works:** "protocol" is a high-trust word in medical contexts. Implies systematic thinking, not impulse.

## 8. "Three things I'd never use as a [profession]…"

- **Profession fit:** dermatologist (skincare), dentist (oral care), nutritionist (supplements).
- **Niche:** anti-recommendation listicle — trust gained by what the figure refuses to use.
- **Claim discipline:** name three real category-level red flags (sulfates, alcohol-denat, sugar alcohols). Avoid named brands. Avoid "kills you" / "causes cancer" hyperbole.
- **Why it works:** showing what the expert refuses is a stronger trust signal than what they recommend. Pair with one positive recommendation in the back half.

## 9. "Here's what's actually happening when you [behavior / symptom]…"

- **Profession fit:** any. Strongest with dermatologist, GP, sleep specialist.
- **Niche:** mechanism-explainer (the cleanest format match). Works when there's a clear cause → mechanism → outcome story.
- **Claim discipline:** the body delivers actual physiology. If the figure can't name a receptor, a hormone, or a cellular process, this hook reads as fake.
- **Why it works:** "actually" implies the viewer's current understanding is wrong, sets up an explanation as the reward for staying.

## 10. "If I had to pick one [ingredient / supplement / product]…"

- **Profession fit:** nutritionist, dermatologist, pharmacist.
- **Niche:** single-ingredient hero plays. Especially strong for brands with one star ingredient.
- **Claim discipline:** the pick must be specific. "Magnesium" is weak; "magnesium glycinate, specifically the chelated form" is on-format.
- **Why it works:** forced-choice framing makes the recommendation feel earned, not promotional.

## 11. "The reason your [pain state] isn't getting better…"

- **Profession fit:** dermatologist (acne / hyperpigmentation), nutritionist (fatigue / bloat), dentist (sensitivity), GP (sleep).
- **Niche:** "you're doing it wrong" reframe — works when the user's existing routine has a common gap.
- **Claim discipline:** the gap must be named specifically in the body (under-dosing, wrong order, missing co-factor). Avoid blanket "you've been lied to" framing.
- **Why it works:** addresses the lurker who already tried something and got nowhere — high-intent.

## 12. "What I take every morning, as a [profession]…"

- **Profession fit:** nutritionist, GP, pharmacist. Weakest with dentist (no breakfast supplement story).
- **Niche:** stack-disclosure variant. Strong for supplement DTC where the figure walks through their personal routine.
- **Claim discipline:** if the figure is AI-generated, this hook crosses a soft line — implies a real personal regimen. Avoid first-person narrative claims that imply a specific real person; keep the regimen generic.
- **Why it works:** social-proof + authority. "If the expert takes it, it's vetted."

## Hook-to-niche quick map

| Niche | Strongest hooks | Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Skincare (acne, aging, hyperpigmentation) | 1, 2, 5, 8, 11 | 6 (no prescriptions in skincare) |
| Supplements / nutra | 5, 7, 9, 10, 12 | 4 (category-stop is risky in supplements) |
| Dental / oral care | 1, 4, 8, 11 | 6, 12 |
| Wellness / sleep / stress | 2, 7, 9, 10 | 6 |
| Cardiometabolic | 6, 7, 9 | 12 (regulatory risk) |
