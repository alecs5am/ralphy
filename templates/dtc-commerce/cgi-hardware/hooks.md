# Hooks — CGI Hardware

The hook is the first 1-2 seconds. In hardware cinema, the hook is almost always **a lighting move + a single product gesture**, not a camera stunt. The viewer's brain commits when it parses "this object is real, expensive, and being treated with respect". Pick one technique below, write it as the opening sentence of the prompt, then build the rest of the spot around it.

**Rule:** the hook describes what the viewer sees in the first frame and what changes by the second frame. It is visual, lit, and tactile — never abstract.

---

## 1. Drop into pitch-black with rim-light reveal

**Setup.** Frame is 100% black. Product is silhouetted, barely visible. A rim light fades up from 30° behind, drawing the silhouette in a single luminous line — then a key light snaps on and the materiality reveals.

**Lighting cue.** Rim from rear-30° fades 0-1s; key (3200K hard) snaps on at 1.0s; fill (1/8 intensity, 7000K) follows half a beat behind.

**Camera.** Locked center, no motion. Or extremely slow push-in (0.1 m/sec).

**Why it works.** Maximum contrast, maximum suspense. The Apple / Bang & Olufsen / luxury-watch playbook.

**Example.** "Pure black frame. A faint cyan rim traces the outline of the device. At 1 second, a hard warm key strikes from the upper-left, raking across the CNC chamfer and revealing brushed-aluminum surface."

---

## 2. Surface-texture macro pull-back

**Setup.** Open at extreme macro — anodizing grain, brushed-steel pattern, leather pores, or glass micro-imperfections fill the frame. Camera pulls back smoothly, scale resolving from "this could be anything" to "this is the device".

**Lighting cue.** Hard side-light maintained throughout — the texture is the subject, light grazing it at 80°.

**Camera.** Dolly back 0.4-0.6m over 2.5s, ease-out. Focus stays sharp throughout.

**Why it works.** Materiality first, identity second. Tells the viewer "look how detailed this is" before they even know what they're looking at.

**Example.** "Extreme macro of brushed-aluminum micro-scratches in raking 80° light. Camera pulls back smoothly over 2.5 seconds, revealing the full back face of the device."

---

## 3. Port-detail orbit

**Setup.** Open on a port, button, or connector — USB-C, headphone jack, antenna stub, dial. Camera orbits 90° around the detail, revealing the surrounding form.

**Lighting cue.** Soft 3-point with strong rim from the orbit-end side, so the silhouette strengthens as the camera arrives.

**Camera.** Slow orbit at 30°/sec, constant distance ~15cm from subject, ease-in/ease-out, lens 50-85mm equivalent.

**Why it works.** Engineers love this; consumers parse it as "they care about every connector". A trust hook.

**Example.** "Macro on a recessed USB-C port, the chamfered cutout catching cyan rim-light. Camera orbits 90° clockwise around the corner, revealing the full edge profile."

---

## 4. Logo-engraving rake-light reveal

**Setup.** Frame holds on a flat surface — back of a phone, lid of a synth, top of a watch. A logo or model-engraving is invisible at first. Light rakes across the surface from a low angle, and the engraving emerges as shadow lines.

**Lighting cue.** Single hard key starting at 90° (parallel to surface, invisible), animating to 70° over 1.5s — the moment the angle drops, the engraving casts shadow and "appears".

**Camera.** Locked. The light does the work.

**Why it works.** Pure industrial-design poetry. Dieter-Rams-clean.

**Example.** "Flat brushed-aluminum surface, light source parallel and invisible. Over 1.5 seconds the key light angle drops from 90° to 70°, and a precision-engraved logo emerges in raking-shadow detail."

---

## 5. Disassembly explode-view

**Setup.** Product hovers center-frame, intact. Components separate outward in zero-gravity — back panel, battery, board, screen, frame — each drifting to its position.

**Lighting cue.** Soft studio 3-point so each component reads clearly. Subtle rim on each piece.

**Camera.** Slow orbit at 20°/sec or locked with internal motion, depending on density.

**Why it works.** Curiosity payoff. Used by Teardown YouTube creators and now expected as a premium-tech beat.

**Example.** "The intact device hovers center-frame against deep grey. Over 2 seconds, components separate outward in zero-gravity: back panel drifts up-right, motherboard floats forward, battery slides down-left, screen glides up-left."

---

## 6. Hand-pick-up reveal

**Setup.** Product rests on a surface — desk, marble pedestal, neutral platform. A hand enters frame and picks it up, lifts it toward the lens. The "human-scale" moment.

**Lighting cue.** Warm 3000K key from upper-left simulating window light. Cool 6500K fill. The hand should be lit identically to the product (no green-screen tell).

**Camera.** Locked or extremely slight push-in. The hand carries the motion.

**Why it works.** Establishes scale and tactility. The viewer projects themselves onto the hand — "this could be in my hand".

**Example.** "Device rests on a brushed-concrete surface in soft window light. A hand enters from frame-right, fingers settle around the chassis, and lifts the device toward the lens, rotating it 30° to a three-quarter view."

---

## 7. Comparison-scale shadow

**Setup.** Product casts a long, sharp shadow on a clean surface. The shadow conveys size before the product itself reads. Camera reframes to bring the product into hero composition.

**Lighting cue.** Single hard key from low side-angle (15-20° above horizontal), creating a shadow 2-3× the product's footprint.

**Camera.** Slow lateral track from shadow-tip toward product, 0.3 m/sec, ease-out.

**Why it works.** Implies physicality and scale. Also visually striking — the shadow does graphic-design work.

**Example.** "Long, sharp shadow stretches across a polished concrete floor. Camera tracks slowly along the shadow toward its source, revealing the device standing in dramatic side-light."

---

## 8. Materiality close-up (aluminum / glass / leather)

**Setup.** Three-or-four split macros of the product's hero surfaces, each held for 1-1.5s, connected by rack-focus or smooth match-cut. Aluminum chamfer → glass back → leather strap → screen pixel-grid.

**Lighting cue.** Each surface gets the light it needs — hard side for metal, sharp Fresnel for glass, warm grazing for leather, even-frontal for screen.

**Camera.** Each beat is locked or has only millimeter drift. Cuts/dissolves carry the energy.

**Why it works.** Proves multi-material craftsmanship. Used by Apple keynote videos almost every year.

**Example.** "Macro 1: brushed-aluminum chamfer in raking light, 1.2s. Rack-focus to macro 2: glass back with sharp Fresnel and faint chromatic dispersion at edges, 1.2s. Match-cut to macro 3: leather strap stitching, warm grazing light revealing grain, 1.2s."

---

## 9. Color-cycle through variants

**Setup.** Product holds hero pose. The chassis color cycles through the available colorways — black → silver → blue → red — each transition a smooth dissolve, not a hard cut.

**Lighting cue.** Lighting holds steady. Only the chassis material/albedo changes.

**Camera.** Locked center, hero angle. No motion.

**Why it works.** Communicates the SKU lineup in a single beat without splitting screen real estate. Nothing-Phone / iPhone-pro launch beat.

**Example.** "Device locked in three-quarter hero pose under soft studio 3-point light. Chassis color cycles: graphite → champagne-gold → midnight-blue → sangria-red, each transition a 0.6-second cross-dissolve."

---

## 10. Glow-on at boot

**Setup.** Product is dark, screen / glyph / LED ring is off. At a designated frame, the display glows on — boot logo, glyph pattern, indicator LED — illuminating the surrounding chassis from within.

**Lighting cue.** Ambient lighting is moody and dim. The glow itself becomes the dominant light source for the hero frame.

**Camera.** Locked or slow push-in to coincide with the glow.

**Why it works.** Self-illumination is high-perceived-value. Nothing Phone glyph, Mac sleep-pulse, watch always-on. The product becomes its own light source.

**Example.** "Device sits in a dim graphite environment, screen black. At 1.8 seconds, the OLED glows on with the boot logo, casting cyan light onto the surrounding chassis chamfer and back-glass."

---

## 11. Profile silhouette into three-quarter

**Setup.** Open on pure side-profile silhouette — the thinness, the curvature, the proportions read as graphic shape. Product rotates slowly to the three-quarter hero angle, depth and material revealing as it turns.

**Lighting cue.** Strong rim only at the start (silhouette). Key fades in as the rotation begins, fill follows.

**Camera.** Locked center. Product rotates on invisible axis.

**Why it works.** Industrial-design fetishization of profile. The Jony-Ive playbook for thin / curved hardware.

**Example.** "Pure black frame. Device shows in side-profile silhouette, rim-lit by a single back light revealing only the curved edge. Over 2 seconds it rotates to three-quarter front, key light blooming as it turns to reveal full materiality."

---

## 12. Macro of moving part (button click, dial, switch)

**Setup.** Extreme close-up on a single mechanical element — a button being pressed, a dial detenting, a switch flipping, a magnet snapping into a dock. Slow-motion captures the mechanism.

**Lighting cue.** Hard key from above-side to throw shadow at the actuation, soft fill to keep the mechanism legible.

**Camera.** Locked, lens 100mm macro equivalent. Or extremely slight push-in synced with the actuation.

**Why it works.** Tactile honesty. The viewer can feel the click. This is the beat that converts on "feel" rather than "look".

**Example.** "Macro on a knurled volume dial under hard side-light. Over 1.5 seconds, the dial rotates one detent click clockwise, the knurling catching micro-highlights, a soft tactile thock landing at the moment of detent."

## Picking a hook

| Hardware category | Strongest hooks |
|---|---|
| Phone | 1 (rim reveal), 8 (materiality), 10 (glow-on), 11 (profile silhouette) |
| Wearable (watch / band / ring) | 6 (hand-pickup), 8 (materiality), 11 (profile silhouette), 12 (moving part) |
| Camera | 3 (port orbit), 5 (explode-view), 8 (materiality), 12 (dial click) |
| Dev-board / SBC | 2 (texture pull-back), 3 (port orbit), 5 (explode-view), 12 (button click) |
| Audio device (synth / DAC / headphones) | 4 (engraving rake), 5 (explode-view), 9 (color-cycle), 12 (dial detent) |
| Appliance | 1 (rim reveal), 6 (hand-pickup), 7 (shadow scale), 10 (glow-on) |
| Power-tool | 2 (texture pull-back), 6 (hand-pickup), 7 (shadow scale), 12 (trigger pull) |
| Dock / charger | 3 (port orbit), 12 (magnet-snap), 10 (glow-on) |
| Peripheral (keyboard / mouse / mic) | 4 (engraving rake), 9 (color-cycle), 12 (key feel) |

**Default if uncertain:** Hook 1 (rim-light reveal) for any premium positioning. Hook 8 (materiality close-up) when the product's strength is multi-material craftsmanship. Hook 12 (moving part) when the product's strength is tactile feel.

**Combine sparingly.** A 12s spot can use one hook for Act 1 and a different macro beat (often Hook 12) inside Act 2. Three or more hooks in one spot reads as confused.
