# Storytime — pre-hook patterns

The 0-2s pre-hook is the single highest-leverage line in the format. It's a contract: "stay 90 seconds and I'll deliver a specific payoff." Generic hooks ("hey guys so something happened…") collapse the format before it starts.

Twelve patterns below. Each lists the literal hook structure, what the setup must promise, an escalation hint, and the niches it lands in.

---

## 1. "I can't believe this actually happened…"

**Hook line.** "I can't believe this actually happened to me at [place]."

**Setup must promise.** A specific event in a specific environment. The viewer's brain commits because the hook signals "absurd-but-true."

**Escalation hint.** Three beats: the moment the situation becomes weird → the moment it becomes wrong → the moment it becomes unbelievable.

**Niche fit.** Dating, work-drama, travel, customer-service horror.

---

## 2. "So my [coworker / friend / mom] did the most insane thing"

**Hook line.** "So my [role] did the most insane thing yesterday and I have to tell someone."

**Setup must promise.** Another character whose behavior is the engine of the story. The narrator is the witness, not the protagonist.

**Escalation hint.** Four beats: normal-baseline of the third party → the first weird thing → escalation → the line they crossed.

**Niche fit.** Family drama, work drama, friend-group politics, parenting (kid as the "third party").

---

## 3. "Storytime: the time I [verb-past]…"

**Hook line.** "Storytime: the time I got fired for sending one email."

**Setup must promise.** A confessional. The narrator owns the chaos. Self-deprecating tone reads as honest.

**Escalation hint.** Three beats: the inciting decision → the consequence → the final humiliation or vindication.

**Niche fit.** Work confessions, school throwbacks, dating fiascos, financial-mistake stories.

---

## 4. "You will not believe what happened to me at [place]"

**Hook line.** "You will not believe what happened to me at the dentist last week."

**Setup must promise.** A boring/safe place where chaos broke out. The mismatch between expected normalcy and actual events is the engine.

**Escalation hint.** The contrast widens with each beat — the more mundane the setting reads at the start, the more outrageous each turn lands.

**Niche fit.** Public-place chaos, customer-service stories, medical-appointment drama, school events.

---

## 5. "Hot off the press, here's what just happened"

**Hook line.** "Hot off the press, this just happened to me five minutes ago."

**Setup must promise.** Real-time recency. The narrator filmed the video while still emotionally activated. Selfie energy must read raw, not staged.

**Escalation hint.** Linear chronology — "okay so I walked in, then she said, then I said, then —" the rawness carries the tension.

**Niche fit.** Live-reaction storytime, dating updates, retail horror, neighbor drama.

---

## 6. "I just got off the phone with [person] and I'm shaking"

**Hook line.** "I just got off the phone with my landlord and I am shaking right now."

**Setup must promise.** A high-stakes phone call whose contents are about to be revealed. The trembling-narrator energy buys 30s of patience.

**Escalation hint.** Quote-the-call beats — "and then she said —" each line worse than the last.

**Niche fit.** Landlord drama, customer-service escalation, family-conflict calls, employer disputes.

---

## 7. "I was today years old when I found out…"

**Hook line.** "I was today years old when I found out my husband has been doing this for ten years."

**Setup must promise.** A reveal-of-something-long-hidden. The hook positions the narrator as the unsuspecting one; the audience braces for the discovery.

**Escalation hint.** Pre-discovery normalcy → the moment of suspicion → the discovery → the reaction.

**Niche fit.** Family secrets, marriage stories, true crime (personal proximity), workplace deceit.

---

## 8. "Okay this is going to sound fake but I swear it happened"

**Hook line.** "Okay this is going to sound fake but I swear on my life it happened."

**Setup must promise.** An implausibly absurd story. The pre-emptive disclaimer signals "the story is so wild you'll doubt me — stay anyway."

**Escalation hint.** Each beat is more implausible than the last. The narrator periodically re-acknowledges the absurdity ("I know, I know — wait for it").

**Niche fit.** True crime, paranormal, travel-horror, once-in-a-lifetime coincidences.

---

## 9. "I'm never going back to [place / person / situation] and here's why"

**Hook line.** "I'm never going back to that gym and here's why."

**Setup must promise.** A grievance story with a clear final-decision payoff. The hook itself reveals the ending; the body explains it.

**Escalation hint.** The breaking-point structure — three frustrations of growing severity, then the moment that broke it.

**Niche fit.** Service complaints, relationship endings, job-quitting stories, neighborhood drama.

---

## 10. "POV: you find out your [relation] has been [doing X]"

**Hook line.** "POV: you find out your boyfriend's mother has been reading your texts."

**Setup must promise.** A second-person framing that puts the viewer in the discovery moment. Reads as relatable even if the specific scenario is rare.

**Escalation hint.** Discovery → confrontation prep → confrontation → fallout.

**Niche fit.** Dating, family-in-law drama, roommate stories, friend-group betrayal.

---

## 11. "Listen — I have to tell you what just happened in [class / meeting / venue]"

**Hook line.** "Listen, I have to tell you what just happened in my 9 AM meeting."

**Setup must promise.** Witnessed-event reportage. The narrator is the camera; the action belongs to others.

**Escalation hint.** Setup the cast (3-5 lines) → the inciting moment → the meltdown → the aftermath.

**Niche fit.** Office storytime, classroom throwbacks, podcast clips, public-event chaos.

---

## 12. "Three things I learned from the worst [event] of my life"

**Hook line.** "Three things I learned from the worst date of my life."

**Setup must promise.** A retrospective lesson-pack wrapped in story. Hybrid of storytime and listicle — works because the listicle promise structures attention while the story carries emotion.

**Escalation hint.** Three story-beats, each anchored to one "lesson." The third lesson is the punchline.

**Niche fit.** Dating retrospectives, financial lessons, parenting realizations, career pivots.

---

## Hook design checklist (apply to any pattern above)

- **Specific noun in the first sentence.** "Airport," "9 AM meeting," "my landlord," "the dentist." Specificity = stickiness.
- **Implied tension.** The hook hints at conflict without resolving it. ("She did the most insane thing" — *what thing?*)
- **Time anchor.** "Yesterday," "five minutes ago," "today years old," "last week." Recency boosts retention.
- **No filler.** "Hey guys, so um, this is kind of a long one but —" → cut. The first 2 seconds are too expensive for hedging.
- **Match the tone declared in the brief.** Comedic story → playful hook. True-crime story → grave hook. Mismatch breaks the contract.

---

## When the hook fails

If the first version of the pre-hook reads flat, the rewrite move is almost always one of three:

1. **Add a specific noun.** "Something weird happened" → "Something weird happened at the airport."
2. **Add a time anchor.** "Let me tell you about my coworker" → "Let me tell you what my coworker did this morning."
3. **Add an implied stakes clause.** "I went on a date" → "I went on a date and I'm still recovering."

If none of those land, the story itself is probably too thin — kick back to the scenarist.
