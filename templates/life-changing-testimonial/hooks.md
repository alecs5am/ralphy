# Life-Changing Testimonial — hooks

12 pain hooks for the 0-3s opener. Each is a setup that the rest of the script must pay off with specific numbers + a discovery moment + a soft CTA. The hook's job is to make the viewer recognize their own pain in the first 3 seconds. If they don't, they scroll.

**Claim discipline (read this first).** A peer testimonial making medical claims it can't back is an FTC problem and a trust failure. Keep it experiential ("my skin felt better", "I sleep through the night now") — not therapeutic ("cured my acne", "fixed my insomnia"). When in doubt, soften.

---

## 1. "I struggled with X for Y years"

**Setup.** Speaker straight to camera, warm light, slightly tired tone. "I struggled with cystic acne for seven years."
**Niche fit.** Skincare, nutra, fitness, mental wellness — any chronic-pain niche.
**Why it stops scroll.** Specificity (seven years, not "a long time") + the duration signals real lived experience. Viewers with the same pain self-recognize instantly.
**Claim discipline.** "Struggled with" is safe. "Suffered from [diagnosis]" only if the speaker has the diagnosis. "Cured my [condition]" — never.

## 2. "I tried everything for X"

**Setup.** "I tried every cream, every dermatologist, every elimination diet — nothing worked."
**Niche fit.** Skincare, nutra, weight-loss, sleep, productivity software.
**Why it stops scroll.** The "I tried everything" frame validates the viewer's own frustration with past attempts and pre-positions the product as the thing that finally worked.
**Claim discipline.** Listing past failed categories is safe. Naming specific competitor products that "didn't work" is legally risky — keep competitors generic ("other brands", "what my doctor recommended").

## 3. "Nothing worked until…"

**Setup.** "Nothing worked. Until my sister sent me a link."
**Niche fit.** Universal. Especially strong for nutra, skincare, fitness.
**Why it stops scroll.** The trailing "until…" creates an open loop the viewer needs closed — they keep watching to find out what worked.
**Claim discipline.** Don't promise the product is universally effective. "Worked for me" is the limit. "Will work for you" — never.

## 4. "I almost gave up on X"

**Setup.** "I almost gave up on ever having clear skin." / "I was about to cancel my business."
**Niche fit.** Skincare, fitness, nutra, software/business, education.
**Why it stops scroll.** Resignation is a powerful emotional anchor — it signals the speaker had tried hard and was at the end of the rope, which makes the eventual win feel earned.
**Claim discipline.** Safe. Just don't pair with a hard medical claim downstream.

## 5. "X used to control my life"

**Setup.** "My acne controlled my life. I cancelled dates. I took sick days. I avoided cameras."
**Niche fit.** Skincare, mental wellness, joint pain, body-image fitness niches.
**Why it stops scroll.** Specific micro-consequences (cancelled dates, sick days) make the pain visceral. Vague pain doesn't convert; specific consequences do.
**Claim discipline.** The list of consequences must be plausible for the niche. Don't escalate to "I couldn't leave the house" unless the brief actually says that.

## 6. "I cried every day because of X"

**Setup.** "I used to cry every morning looking in the mirror."
**Niche fit.** Skincare, weight, hair, sensitive emotional niches.
**Why it stops scroll.** Highest-emotional-stakes hook in the pack. Use sparingly — overuse reads manipulative. Best with a heartfelt-emotional tone, NOT hyped.
**Claim discipline.** Soft tone, soft CTA. A "cried every day" hook paired with "Click the link, code SAVE20" reads predatory and tanks trust.

## 7. "[Number] years of [pain] and then…"

**Setup.** "Twelve years of joint pain. And then, three weeks ago…"
**Niche fit.** Nutra (joint, sleep, digestion), chronic-condition niches.
**Why it stops scroll.** Numerical specificity in the hook itself. Twelve years + three weeks is concrete enough that the viewer trusts the speaker before the product is even mentioned.
**Claim discipline.** The "three weeks" claim must be true. If the brief doesn't specify a timeframe, soften to "and then I found something that helped."

## 8. "I'm not paid to say this"

**Setup.** "I'm not paid to say this — I just genuinely needed to share it."
**Niche fit.** Universal, but especially nutra, skincare, software where ad-fatigue is high.
**Why it stops scroll.** Direct disarmament of the "this is an ad" reflex. Works because it acknowledges the viewer's skepticism.
**Claim discipline.** **DO NOT USE if the speaker IS paid / sponsored / affiliate.** This is FTC fraud. Use only for genuinely organic UGC reposts or when the testimonial is a creative dramatization clearly labeled as such.

## 9. "Changed my life"

**Setup.** "I don't say this lightly — this product changed my life."
**Niche fit.** Universal. Strongest for nutra, skincare, software, education.
**Why it stops scroll.** It's the most overused testimonial line in the format — which means viewers expect it. Underplay it ("I don't say this lightly") to make it land.
**Claim discipline.** Safe as a subjective statement. "Changed my life" is opinion, not a measurable claim.

## 10. "I never thought I'd say this but…"

**Setup.** "I never thought I'd be the person posting a testimonial. But here I am."
**Niche fit.** Universal — especially when the speaker presents as skeptical or analytical.
**Why it stops scroll.** Self-aware framing pre-empts the viewer's "ugh, an ad" reflex. The skeptic-converted arc is high-trust.
**Claim discipline.** Safe. Pair with matter-of-fact-relieved tone, not heartfelt-emotional.

## 11. "[Specific milestone] said this is X"

**Setup.** "My dermatologist told me I'd need accutane forever." / "My trainer said my form was the problem, but I plateaued anyway."
**Niche fit.** Nutra, fitness, skincare, education — any niche with a credentialed authority figure.
**Why it stops scroll.** Authority anchor in the hook builds credibility before the product is mentioned. The implied "and then I found something better" sets up the discovery beat.
**Claim discipline.** **High risk.** Quoting an authority figure who didn't say what's quoted is defamation / fabrication. Only use when the brief explicitly supplies the quote. Soften to "my dermatologist mentioned…" when uncertain.

## 12. "X used to be impossible"

**Setup.** "Walking up the stairs used to be impossible." / "Closing a sales call used to be impossible."
**Niche fit.** Nutra (mobility), fitness, software (productivity), education.
**Why it stops scroll.** Concrete daily-life task as the pain anchor — viewers immediately picture themselves struggling with that task. The implied resolution ("and now it's not") is the open loop.
**Claim discipline.** Safe. Daily-life-task pain is hard to overstate.

---

## Choosing a hook

| Niche | Recommended hooks | Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Skincare (acne / clear skin) | 1, 2, 5, 6, 11 | 8 (unless genuinely organic) |
| Nutra (joint / sleep / digestion) | 1, 3, 7, 11, 12 | 11 if no authority quote |
| Fitness (weight / strength) | 4, 9, 10, 12 | 6 (too heavy for fitness) |
| Software (saved my business) | 4, 8, 9, 10 | 5, 6 (mismatched stakes) |
| Service (changed how I X) | 3, 9, 10 | 6, 11 |
| Education (learned X in N days) | 4, 9, 10, 12 | 5, 6 |

## Master rule

The hook must pay off. A "twelve years of joint pain" opener that's followed by a vague "I feel better now" outcome breaks the contract. Specificity at the start demands specificity at the end. If the user's brief doesn't supply specific outcome numbers, soften the hook — don't write checks the outcome can't cash.
