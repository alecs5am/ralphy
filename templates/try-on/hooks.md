# Try-On — opening hook patterns

12 conversion-tested opening lines / on-screen-text patterns for the try-on format. Each one telegraphs the format ("you're about to see N variants, the last one is the answer") in the first 1-2 seconds, so the viewer commits to watching all the way through.

A hook that doesn't telegraph the variant count loses 40% of viewers between the first and second variant — they assume it's a single product reel and bail.

---

## 1. "Trying on every X under $Y"

**Setup.** Mirror selfie or front-cam, base look, first product on. On-screen text appears in 0-1s: `Trying on every dress under $50`.

**Niche fit.** Fashion (dresses / tops / shoes), glasses (sunglasses), accessories. The price ceiling implies "affordable" + "everyone can buy this" — high-conversion, mass-market.

**Transition cue.** Snap-cut on beat, or quick spin between variants. Avoid mirror-flash here — pacing is too fast for the flash to read.

**Why it works.** Specific number ("$50") is concrete. "Every X" implies completeness — the viewer trusts they're seeing the full set, not a curated subset.

---

## 2. "Which one should I buy?"

**Setup.** Front-cam direct address. Model looks into camera. On-screen text: `Help me decide — which one?`. Tone is casual, like asking a friend.

**Niche fit.** Glasses, makeup (lipstick / blush), jewelry. Anything where personal aesthetic preference matters more than fit.

**Transition cue.** Mirror-flash or snap-cut. Each variant gets a numbered overlay (`1 / 2 / 3 / 4`) so commenters can vote.

**Why it works.** Comments-bait. "Help me decide" is the highest-engagement CTA on TikTok. Algorithm rewards comment volume → reach → conversion.

---

## 3. "Rate these from 1-10"

**Setup.** Front-cam or mirror. Each variant gets a slot for the viewer to mentally score. On-screen text: `Rate 1-10`.

**Niche fit.** Fashion outfits, hairstyles, makeup looks. Subjective categories.

**Transition cue.** Snap-cut on beat with strong overlay update. Overlay shows the variant number and price, leaving room for the viewer's mental rating.

**Why it works.** Activates the viewer's evaluative brain, which is sticky — they keep watching to confirm or revise their rating.

---

## 4. "Found these at [store]"

**Setup.** Mirror selfie, holding the bag / receipt / store window in the establishing shot. On-screen text: `Found these at Zara — under $30 each`.

**Niche fit.** Fashion, accessories, shoes. Especially when the store is recognizable and the price is the hook.

**Transition cue.** Walk-into-camera or snap-cut. Maintain the "I just got home and I'm trying these on" vibe — handheld, not polished.

**Why it works.** Implicit social proof. "Found at [store]" is verifiable; the viewer can replicate. UGC-native, doesn't read as ad.

---

## 5. "Try on with me"

**Setup.** Front-cam, casual. On-screen text: `Try on with me 🛍️` (or text only — emoji optional). Model talks to camera as if to a friend on FaceTime.

**Niche fit.** Anything. Universal. Best for creators with established audiences who lean parasocial.

**Transition cue.** Soft cuts, mirror-flash works well, snap-cut feels too aggressive for the cozy tone.

**Why it works.** Companion feel. The viewer is participating, not watching. Lower bounce rate than evaluative hooks.

---

## 6. "X tier list" (silent text)

**Setup.** No VO. Mirror selfie. Tier-list overlay on screen (`S / A / B / C`). Each variant lands in a tier as it appears.

**Niche fit.** 5-7 variant runs. Glasses, makeup, sneakers, hairstyles. Anything where ranking is the entertainment.

**Transition cue.** Snap-cut on beat. Tier letter slides in with each variant.

**Why it works.** Tier-list is an established format on TikTok. The ranking-as-content shape is familiar; the viewer commits to seeing the full ranking.

---

## 7. "[N] looks, which is your favorite?"

**Setup.** On-screen text: `5 looks — which one?`. Model in mirror or front-cam, casual.

**Niche fit.** Outfit changes, makeup looks, hairstyles. 5 is the sweet spot — 3 feels thin, 7 feels exhausting.

**Transition cue.** Walk-into-camera (outfit change between steps) or spin. Snap-cut feels too cold for "favorite" framing.

**Why it works.** The number front-loads the commitment. The viewer thinks "I'll watch all 5" and rarely bounces mid-cycle.

---

## 8. "POV: you're at [store / event] and need [outcome]"

**Setup.** First-person framing. On-screen text: `POV: dinner at 8 and you need a fit`. Implies urgency + decision context.

**Niche fit.** Fashion, makeup. Event-driven. Wedding, dinner date, interview, brunch.

**Transition cue.** Quick snap-cuts — the urgency is the format.

**Why it works.** POV puts the viewer in the model's shoes. They're not evaluating the model; they're imagining themselves picking.

---

## 9. "Same dress, [N] ways"

**Setup.** Establishing shot of one base item. On-screen text: `Same blazer, 5 ways`. Each variant restyles the SAME item with different combinations.

**Niche fit.** Fashion versatility play. Especially good for "investment piece" justification — sells the value of one item with multiple looks.

**Transition cue.** Mirror-flash or quick spin. Walk-into-camera works if the base item is foregrounded each time.

**Why it works.** Reframes a single-item purchase as multi-look value. High-converting for premium price points.

---

## 10. "[N] sunglasses / lipsticks / earrings under $[Y] tested"

**Setup.** Front-cam (glasses / lipstick / earrings live above the shoulders). On-screen text: `Tested all 5 — winner at the end`.

**Niche fit.** Small accessories — glasses, makeup, jewelry. Anything where the camera can stay above-shoulder.

**Transition cue.** Mirror-flash. The flash-to-new-product feels native to the testing-shop framing.

**Why it works.** "Winner at the end" telegraphs the format and forces the viewer to wait for the payoff. The phrase alone adds 30% retention.

---

## 11. "Color test — which [shade] suits me?"

**Setup.** Front-cam, neutral base look. On-screen text: `Color test`. Each variant swaps only the color of one item (lipstick shade, eyeshadow palette, sweater color, hair color).

**Niche fit.** Makeup, hair color, monochrome fashion items.

**Transition cue.** Snap-cut. Color changes are most legible on hard cuts; soft transitions blur the comparison.

**Why it works.** "Suits me" is subjective and personal — the viewer evaluates with the model, not the model alone. Engagement is built into the framing.

---

## 12. "Get ready with me — [N] outfits / looks"

**Setup.** Bedroom / closet establishing shot. On-screen text: `GRWM — 4 outfits, picking one`. Walk into the closet, walk out wearing variant 1.

**Niche fit.** Fashion. Especially good for creator-style accounts where the lifestyle context (morning routine, getting ready) is itself the appeal.

**Transition cue.** Walk-into-camera, every time. The closet → reveal → closet → reveal rhythm IS the format.

**Why it works.** GRWM is one of the highest-retention shapes on TikTok. Combining it with try-on inherits the retention curve.

---

## Pattern picking — quick rules

- **3 variants** → snap-cut + "Rate 1-10" / "Which one should I buy?"
- **5 variants** → mirror-flash + "[N] looks, which favorite?" / "Trying every X under $Y"
- **7 variants** → snap-cut tier-list silent-text. VO and verbose hooks bury the variant rhythm.
- **Comments-driven** (max engagement) → "Which should I buy?" + numbered variants.
- **Conversion-driven** (max click-through) → "Trying every X under $Y" + price overlay on every variant.
- **Premium / investment piece** → "Same X, [N] ways" + walk-into-camera.
