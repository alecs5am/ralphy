# Title-banner hook patterns — Podcast Clip

The title-banner is the hook. It sits at the top 12% of the frame for 0-2.4s (slide-in 0.4s, hold 1.6s, fade 0.4s). It must promise something specific. Vague banners ("interesting moment", "great point") kill the cut — the viewer scrolls before the speaker gets to the payoff.

12 patterns below, each pulled from real viral podcast clips. For each: setup, when-to-use, banner-design notes (font, color, position).

---

## 1. "[Guest] reveals the truth about X"

**Setup.** The guest is widely known but the topic is one they rarely speak about publicly. The clip is the moment they break that silence.

**When to use.** Interview format. The moment must contain a definitive statement, not a hedge. "I'll tell you what really happened" beats "well, you know, it was complicated."

**Banner design.** Hormozi-yellow background (#FFD500), black text, Inter Bold 70px, 2 lines max. Drop-shadow 4px offset, 8px blur. Slide in from top.

**Examples.**
- "Sam Altman reveals the truth about GPT-5"
- "Mike Tyson reveals the truth about his prison years"

---

## 2. "X tells [Host] something INSANE"

**Setup.** All-caps INSANE / WILD / CRAZY at the end. Implies the host themselves is shocked, transferring the shock to the viewer.

**When to use.** When the clip has visible host reaction (raised eyebrows, a "what" laugh, leaning back). The reaction shot is what sells it.

**Banner design.** Hormozi-yellow. INSANE in 80px (10px larger than the rest), still Inter Bold. The size jump on the kicker word is the visual hook.

**Examples.**
- "Elon tells Joe something INSANE"
- "Ex-CIA agent tells Lex something WILD"

---

## 3. "The most controversial thing [Guest] has ever said"

**Setup.** Superlative framing. "Most controversial / dangerous / honest / unfiltered." The clip itself must deliver — if the take is mild, the banner over-promises and the cut flops.

**When to use.** Hot-take moments where the guest takes a clear, divisive position. Works especially well with public figures who are usually media-trained and "go off-script" here.

**Banner design.** Quote-card style. White background, black serif (Playfair Display Bold 60px), 3 lines including attribution. Slide-in from left.

**Examples.**
- "The most controversial thing Tucker has ever said about the war"
- "The most honest answer Bezos has ever given about Amazon workers"

---

## 4. "Why [Guest] thinks [hot-take]"

**Setup.** Direct exposure of the take in the banner. The viewer reads the take in 1 second; the cut is the supporting argument.

**When to use.** When the take is short and specific. "Why Naval thinks remote work will die" works. "Why Naval thinks society is changing" doesn't — too vague.

**Banner design.** Minimal-white. White Inter Bold 65px on a 60% black gradient bar. The take in italics. Slide-in from right.

**Examples.**
- "Why Naval thinks crypto is mostly fraud"
- "Why Cuban thinks Shark Tank is rigged"

---

## 5. Direct quote-card (no framing)

**Setup.** The banner IS the quote. Speaker's exact words, in quotes, with attribution below. No "Why X thinks" wrapper.

**When to use.** When the line itself is so good it stands alone. One-liners, punchlines, contrarian declarations.

**Banner design.** Quote-card style. White background, black serif Playfair Display Bold 55px, attribution line in 30px gray italic ("— Guest Name") below. Centered. Crossfade in (no slide).

**Examples.**
- > "Most CEOs are just expensive secretaries."
  > — Reid Hoffman
- > "I lost $40 million in one day and slept fine."
  > — Some founder

---

## 6. "[Guest]'s reaction to [event] is wild"

**Setup.** The clip is a *reaction* shot. The guest is reacting to a video / news / question. The banner sets up what they're reacting to.

**When to use.** When the reaction itself is the content (face, tone shift, long pause). Static-talking-head reaction clips need this banner because the visual alone doesn't tell you what they're reacting to.

**Banner design.** Hormozi-yellow. 70px Inter Bold. "is wild / is unbelievable / says everything" as the kicker. Slide-in from top.

**Examples.**
- "Joe's reaction to the Epstein video is wild"
- "Lex's reaction to GPT-7 says everything"

---

## 7. "Nobody wants to talk about this, but [Guest] did"

**Setup.** Establishes the topic as forbidden / suppressed before showing the speaker breaking the silence. The frame "nobody wants to talk about" is doing all the work.

**When to use.** Topics with social or political risk. The clip itself can be moderate — the framing makes it feel transgressive.

**Banner design.** Minimal-white on a near-black (90% black) bar. Inter Bold 60px. Italic on "this." Slide-in from bottom.

**Examples.**
- "Nobody wants to talk about this, but Peterson did"
- "Nobody wants to talk about this on Wall Street, but Burry did"

---

## 8. "[Guest] disagrees with [Authority figure / consensus]"

**Setup.** Conflict / contrast banner. The viewer wants to see one specific person disagree with another specific person. Two-name banner.

**When to use.** Debates, panels, interviews where the guest takes an explicit anti-consensus stance against a named opponent.

**Banner design.** Hormozi-yellow with two-tone (yellow / black-half) split. Both names in 65px Inter Bold ExtraBold. "vs." in 50px between them. Slide-in from top.

**Examples.**
- "Musk disagrees with Altman on AI safety"
- "Buffett vs. Ackman on Berkshire's next move"

---

## 9. Number-anchored ("[Guest]'s 3-step rule for X")

**Setup.** Numerical promise — "3 rules", "5 mistakes", "7 questions". Triggers a list-following reflex; viewer stays for the count.

**When to use.** Practical / instructional clips. Founder advice, productivity, fitness, finance. Don't use for emotional / dramatic moments — feels too clinical.

**Banner design.** Quote-card style. White background. Number in red Inter Black 90px, rest of the line in black Inter Bold 55px. Crossfade in.

**Examples.**
- "Naval's 3-step rule for staying focused"
- "Buffett's 5 questions before any investment"

---

## 10. "What [Guest] said when asked about X"

**Setup.** Implies a question was asked off-screen. Sets the cut up as a direct answer to a specific prompt the viewer wants the answer to.

**When to use.** Q&A formats, podcast interviews where the host's question is short and the answer is the meat. Works best when the question itself is provocative (banner) and the answer is non-obvious (clip).

**Banner design.** Minimal-white on 70% black bar. Inter Bold 60px. The question itself in italics on line 2 ("when asked: '...'"). Slide-in from right.

**Examples.**
- "What Bezos said when asked about retirement"
- "What Putin said when asked about the war ending"

---

## 11. Cliffhanger banner ("Wait for it...")

**Setup.** The banner promises a payoff at the end of the clip, not the start. The viewer watches all 30-50 seconds to see what happens.

**When to use.** Moments where the build-up matters. Long setup → punchline. Story arcs in the source material.

**Banner design.** Hormozi-yellow. 70px Inter Bold. "Wait for it..." in 80px italic at line 2 (kicker). The "..." is the visual cue. Slide-in from top, longer hold (3.0s instead of 1.6s).

**Examples.**
- "Joe asks Tyson the wrong question — wait for it..."
- "Founder pitches Mark Cuban — wait for it..."

---

## 12. Simple punchline-as-banner

**Setup.** No framing, no quote marks. Just the punchline of the clip, telegraphed in the banner. The clip is the setup; the banner is the payoff already.

**When to use.** Counter-intuitive — only works when the clip is so dense / fast that the punchline-spoiler is itself the hook (the viewer wants to see HOW the speaker arrives at it). High-skill pattern.

**Banner design.** Minimal-white. Inter Bold 70px on 50% black gradient. No attribution. Crossfade in.

**Examples.**
- "He fired his entire C-suite in one day."
- "She closed the round in 47 minutes."

---

## Banner copy rules (apply to all 12 patterns)

- **Max 2 lines, ever.** 3-line banners cover too much frame and lose the speaker's eyes.
- **Max 60 characters total.** Beyond that, the font has to shrink below 60px and stops being readable on a phone in 1 second.
- **No questions ending in "?"** — questions feel weak. Convert to a declarative or a "Why X thinks Y" framing.
- **Never use the word "amazing", "incredible", "you won't believe"** — kills credibility, signals clickbait, hurts retention beyond the first 3s.
- **Specificity > intensity.** "Tyson on prison food" beats "Tyson reveals SHOCKING TRUTH". The specific detail is what makes it feel real.
- **Match banner style to caption style.** Hormozi-yellow banner pairs with Hormozi-impact captions. Quote-card banner pairs with karaoke or TikTok-white. Don't mix Hormozi banner with minimal captions — looks like two different videos.
